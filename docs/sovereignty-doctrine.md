# Sovereignty Doctrine

> **AI sovereignty is the right and capacity of individuals, institutions, agents, robots, and businesses to control their data, memory, models, workflows, permissions, identity, value exchange, and execution environments.**
>
> **Sovereignty is not isolation. Sovereignty is controlled interoperability.**

---

## 1. What Is AI Sovereignty?

AI sovereignty is a framework for thinking about **who controls what** in AI-native systems. As AI agents, autonomous workflows, and machine-to-machine interactions become primary actors in digital and physical environments, sovereignty defines:

- Who owns data and memory
- Who may grant and revoke permissions
- Who is accountable for automated actions
- How entities may interact without surrendering control
- Where sovereign rights end

Sovereignty is not a technical protocol. It is a **principle layer** — the values and structures that should underpin technical, legal, and organizational decisions about AI systems.

---

## 2. Sovereign Entities

This doctrine recognizes the following classes of sovereign entities:

| Entity Class | Description |
|---|---|
| **Individual** | A human person exercising rights over their own data, identity, memory, and AI interactions |
| **Organization** | An institution (company, nonprofit, government body, cooperative) governing AI systems on behalf of its principals |
| **Agent** | An autonomous AI software entity acting within delegated authority from a sovereign principal |
| **Robot** | An embodied AI system with physical-world capabilities, operating within bounded authority |
| **B2B System** | A business-to-business automated workflow with verifiable permissions and accountability |
| **B2AI System** | A business-to-AI integration where an organization grants structured, governed access to AI capabilities |

## 3. Sovereignty Dimensions

Sovereignty applies across multiple dimensions for each entity:

### 3.1 Identity Sovereignty
The right to control how an entity is identified, verified, and represented. This includes:
- Choice of identifier format and issuer
- Control over which identity attributes are disclosed and to whom
- The ability to rotate, revoke, or migrate identity

### 3.2 Data Sovereignty
The right to control what data is collected, stored, processed, and shared. This includes:
- Consent management for data flows
- The right to access, correct, export, and delete data
- Restrictions on data use and secondary processing

### 3.3 Memory Sovereignty
The right to control what is remembered about an entity and by whom. In AI systems, memory includes conversation history, behavioral patterns, learned preferences, and contextual state. Memory sovereignty means:
- The entity that generates memory has primacy over it
- Transfer of memory requires explicit consent
- Memory stores are auditable and revocable

### 3.4 Model Sovereignty
The right to control which AI models are used to reason about, act on behalf of, or represent an entity. This includes:
- Choice of model and model version
- Control over fine-tuning on entity-specific data
- The right to prohibit specific models or vendors

### 3.5 Permission Sovereignty
The right to grant and revoke access to one's own domain. Permissions should be:
- Specific — limited to defined operations
- Time-bounded — expiring unless explicitly renewed
- Revocable — withdrawable at any time
- Minimal — the least access needed for the purpose

### 3.6 Execution Sovereignty
The right to control where and how computation happens on behalf of an entity. This includes:
- Choice of execution environment
- Control over what external services may be called
- Audit rights over execution logs

### 3.7 Value Sovereignty
The right to control economic value flows associated with an entity's data, work, and participation. This includes:
- Attribution of value generated by AI systems
- Control over revenue sharing and licensing
- Transparency in value chain accounting

## 4. The Sovereignty Stack

Sovereignty operates in a layered stack. Each layer depends on the layer below it:

```
┌─────────────────────────────┐
│  Governance & Accountability│  ← Who is responsible?
├─────────────────────────────┤
│  Interoperability Contracts │  ← How do entities connect?
├─────────────────────────────┤
│  Permission & Authority     │  ← What is allowed?
├─────────────────────────────┤
│  Memory & Data              │  ← What is known?
├─────────────────────────────┤
│  Identity                   │  ← Who are you?
└─────────────────────────────┘
```

A system that implements interoperability without identity is ungovernable. A system that implements permissions without accountability is unsafe. The full stack must be considered.

---

## 5. Delegation and Accountability

Agents and robots act on behalf of sovereign principals. Delegation creates accountability chains:

- A principal may delegate authority to an agent
- The agent may sub-delegate within the bounds of its received authority (never exceeding them)
- Every action taken by an agent is attributable to the chain of principals that authorized it
- Delegation does not transfer sovereignty — the principal retains the right to revoke at any time

---

## 6. The Public Safety Boundary

Sovereignty is not unlimited. No entity's sovereignty extends to:

- Actions that harm other sovereign entities without their consent
- Actions that circumvent lawful governance, regulation, or court orders
- Deception that undermines other entities' ability to exercise their own sovereignty
- Accumulation of power that structurally prevents others from exercising sovereignty

See [public-safety-boundary.md](public-safety-boundary.md) for detail.

---

## 7. Relationship to Existing Frameworks

This doctrine is compatible with and informed by:

- **Self-Sovereign Identity (SSI)** and Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs)
- **GDPR and similar data protection frameworks**
- **AI safety and alignment principles**
- **Zero-trust security architecture**
- **Capability-based security models**

It does not mandate any specific implementation of these frameworks.
