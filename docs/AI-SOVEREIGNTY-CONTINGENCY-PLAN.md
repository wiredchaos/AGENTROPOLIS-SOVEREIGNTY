# AGENTROPOLIS AI Sovereignty Contingency Plan

**Status:** Canonical operating doctrine  
**Scope:** Intelligence Grid, districts, applications, agents, models, compute, data, regulation, and jurisdiction  
**Primary rule:** Models are replaceable utilities. Identity, memory, skills, permissions, governance, and execution history remain under AGENTROPOLIS control.

## Mission

AGENTROPOLIS must remain operational if:

- Chinese model providers close weights or restrict exports.
- Western frontier APIs become expensive, restricted, degraded, censored, or unavailable.
- Open-weight development accelerates, stalls, or fragments.
- NVIDIA, Huawei, hyperscalers, or custom silicon vendors create incompatible ecosystems.
- Edge inference overtakes cloud inference, or fails to mature.
- Regulation fragments AI by jurisdiction, capability, or industry.
- Hardware shortages, sanctions, outages, or geopolitical events interrupt access.
- Model quality commoditizes and value moves to agents, workflows, data, and trust.

> Do not choose one future. Build the Intelligence Grid so every future has an entrance and none of them owns the keys.

## 2. Scenario Register

### S01 — Open-weight golden age

**Condition:** Open models approach closed-frontier quality.

**Opportunity:** AGENTROPOLIS becomes the orchestration, memory, governance, and skills layer above interchangeable models.

**Actions:**

- Maintain an open-model evaluation pipeline.
- Support portable runtimes and quantizations.
- Archive permitted weights, licenses, tokenizers, and model cards.
- Separate model adapters from district logic.
- Monetize managed inference, skills, memory, governance, auditability, and execution.

### S02 — Frontier closes, small models stay open

Use an escalation ladder:

1. Deterministic rules and code.
2. Tiny local model.
3. Small local model.
4. Medium private model.
5. Premium frontier API.
6. Multi-model council.
7. Human approval.

### S03 — China closes advanced weights

**Risks:** Foreign access blocks, restrictive licenses, unavailable updates, export reviews, and data-governance conflicts.

**Actions:**

- Mirror legally distributable releases.
- Record checksums, licenses, source URLs, and review dates.
- Maintain Western and local substitutes.
- Do not send regulated or sensitive data to foreign APIs by default.
- Freeze new dependencies when restrictions materially tighten.

**Trigger:** Activate when any two occur:

- Official export-review rules are published.
- A major lab removes public weights.
- Foreign API access becomes region-blocked.
- Commercial licenses materially tighten.
- Downloads require jurisdiction verification.
- Multiple major labs delay open releases simultaneously.

### S04 — China continues aggressive open releases

Use high-value Chinese models where lawful and appropriate, but never exclusively. Every production dependency requires a Western substitute, a local substitute, a degraded deterministic mode, and a documented migration path.

### S05 — NVIDIA becomes the open AI operating system

Adopt NVIDIA optimizations where valuable, but require portability to standard PyTorch, alternate accelerators, CPU fallback, cloud-neutral containers, and exportable configurations.

> Open weights do not equal open infrastructure.

### S06 — Huawei creates a parallel ecosystem

Treat Huawei and Ascend support as optional adapters. Maintain jurisdiction-specific deployments, independent security review, open formats, and separation between model artifacts and hardware-specific compiled artifacts.

### S07 — Closed API dominance

All providers must sit behind the AGENTROPOLIS Model Gateway. Applications never call vendors directly.

Every critical workflow requires:

- Primary provider.
- Secondary provider.
- Local fallback.
- No-model emergency mode.

### S08 — API price war

Use cheap APIs without assuming promotional pricing is permanent. Maintain per-workflow cost telemetry, replacement-cost estimates, caching, token budgets, and automatic routing.

### S09 — Frontier inference becomes expensive

Distill repeated workflows, replace model work with code, retrieve before generating, cache outputs, batch non-urgent work, and require revenue justification for premium calls.

### S10 — Edge AI explodes

Build a Personal City Node with:

- Local encrypted memory.
- Local identity and permissions.
- Offline skills.
- User-selected models.
- Selective cloud escalation.
- Encrypted synchronization.

### S11 — Edge AI stalls

The Personal City Node becomes a secure thin client that preserves local encryption, memory indexing, permissions, caching, and provider control while inference remains remote.

### S12 — Custom silicon fragments the market

Maintain a Hardware Abstraction Registry recording runtime, precision, memory, throughput, latency, energy use, cost, quantization support, limitations, jurisdiction, and vendor dependency.

### S13 — Global AI export war

Operate three separable zones:

- **US/Allied Zone:** compliant Western infrastructure.
- **China-Aligned Zone:** separate providers, keys, storage, and governance.
- **Neutral/Sovereign Zone:** open and locally hosted infrastructure.

No silent movement of secrets, regulated data, keys, or identity stores between incompatible zones.

### S14 — Mandatory model audits

Build model versions, prompt versions, output provenance, tool-call logs, data lineage, approval checkpoints, incident records, evaluation history, rollback support, consent records, and retention controls into the Intelligence Grid.

### S15 — Autonomous agents become licensed

Classify every skill by autonomy tier:

| Tier | Capability |
|---|---|
| A0 | Read-only |
| A1 | Draft or recommend |
| A2 | Execute reversible actions |
| A3 | Financial or contractual action with approval |
| A4 | Limited autonomous operation |
| A5 | Prohibited without specialized governance |

No district may silently upgrade autonomy.

### S16 — Open-model security crisis

Verify checksums and signatures, scan dependencies, sandbox inference, separate reasoning from tool execution, deny network access by default, maintain allowlists, run canary evaluations, and preserve immutable artifacts.

### S17 — Model performance commoditizes

AGENTROPOLIS moat becomes the Intelligence Grid, CHAOS SKILLS, districts, memory, identity, reputation, governance, execution history, data rights, community, and distribution.

### S18 — One provider achieves temporary superiority

Route premium workloads to the leader while keeping prompts, tools, identity, memory, and routing outside the provider. Assume leadership is temporary.

### S19 — AGI-like capability arrives faster than expected

Default-deny new tool capabilities, separate planning from execution, enforce transaction limits, preserve human kill switches, test deception and exfiltration, isolate credentials, restrict self-modification, and require signed governance changes.

### S20 — AI progress slows

Shift investment from benchmark chasing to better workflows, interfaces, data, retrieval, integrations, evaluations, and human-agent collaboration.

### S21 — Decentralized AI succeeds

Use decentralized compute for public inference, rendering, batch jobs, bounties, gaming NPCs, and simulations. Require verifiable execution, escrow, reputation, privacy classification, and fraud controls.

### S22 — Decentralized AI fails

Keep blockchain focused on settlement, escrow, identity attestations, provenance, bounties, ownership, and public audit trails. Do not force ordinary inference onchain.

### S23 — Licenses become the primary weapon

Maintain a Model Rights Registry containing source, version, license, commercial rights, modification rights, redistribution rights, distillation rights, jurisdiction restrictions, acceptable-use limits, attribution duties, termination conditions, and review dates.

### S24 — Advertising and data subsidize AI

Offer a paid private mode with no advertising, no training on user data, local-first memory, exportable identity, transparent costs, and user-selected providers.

### S25 — Providers become full operating systems

Do not compete as another generic assistant. AGENTROPOLIS must own the federation layer across providers, identities, chains, devices, districts, data stores, and portable skills.

## 3. Mandatory Architecture

### Model Gateway

The gateway owns:

- Provider routing.
- Authentication.
- Cost limits.
- Data classification.
- Logging.
- Retry and circuit-breaker logic.
- Model substitution.
- Prompt adaptation.
- Output normalization.
- Policy enforcement.

Required interfaces:

- `generate`
- `reason`
- `embed`
- `rerank`
- `transcribe`
- `synthesize`
- `vision`
- `moderate`
- `code`
- `tool_plan`

### Capability Registry

Track demonstrated capability, not marketing claims:

- Reasoning.
- Coding.
- Tool use.
- Context length.
- Vision and audio.
- Language coverage.
- Cost and latency.
- Reliability.
- Privacy.
- License.
- Hardware requirements.
- Geography.
- Safety profile.

### Evaluation Arena

Every candidate model must run against AGENTROPOLIS-specific tests for district routing, skill activation, structured output, long-running reliability, hallucination resistance, tool selection, financial arithmetic, security behavior, canon preservation, cost per completed task, and latency per completed task.

### Provider Circuit Breaker

Reduce or stop traffic when error rates spike, latency breaches thresholds, prices exceed limits, evaluations regress, terms change, security incidents occur, or geographic access changes.

### Portable Memory Layer

Identity, memory, canon, permissions, audit history, embeddings, and structured knowledge must remain provider-neutral. Re-embedding must be supported when embedding models change.

### Skill Portability

CHAOS SKILLS define inputs, outputs, tools, permissions, evaluations, and required capabilities. They must not depend on a named model unless technically unavoidable.

### Local Survival Node

Minimum emergency capability:

- Search local knowledge.
- Read project canon.
- Route commands.
- Draft text.
- Run approved deterministic tools.
- Export user data.
- Restore cloud connections.

### No-Model Mode

Payments, wallet protections, access control, audit logs, emergency messaging, account export, and governance locks must retain basic functionality without generative AI.

## 4. Operational Alert Levels

### GREEN — Normal operations

Multiple viable providers, stable prices and licenses, and passing fallbacks. Optimize cost and performance.

### YELLOW — Market shift

A major provider closes weights, prices rise materially, licenses tighten, hardware availability worsens, or government controls are proposed. Increase archival and substitution readiness.

### ORANGE — Dependency threat

Multiple providers become unavailable, controls are enacted, core quality drops, hardware prices spike, or terms threaten data ownership. Freeze expansion and migrate new workloads.

### RED — Active disruption

A production provider is blocked, compromised, revoked, or unlawful. Trip the circuit breaker and switch to approved fallbacks.

### BLACK — Systemic crisis

Cloud, hardware, internet, payments, or multiple providers fail simultaneously. Activate sovereign local operation, restrict autonomy, preserve data, and maintain essential services only.
