# The Public Safety Boundary

Sovereignty is not unlimited. This document defines where sovereign rights end — the **public safety boundary** — and why this boundary is essential to a functioning AI-native society.

---

## 1. The Principle

> **No entity's sovereignty extends to actions that harm others, circumvent lawful governance, or structurally prevent others from exercising their own sovereignty.**

Sovereignty is a framework for protecting autonomy and self-determination. A sovereignty framework that allowed any entity to harm others in the name of their own rights would be self-defeating — it would destroy the conditions that make sovereignty meaningful.

---

## 2. What the Public Safety Boundary Prohibits

The following actions are outside the scope of sovereign rights, regardless of the entity attempting to exercise them:

### 2.1 Physical Harm
No sovereign right extends to directing AI systems, agents, or robots to cause physical harm to humans or animals. This includes:
- Programming robots to endanger humans
- Using AI to plan or facilitate violence
- Deploying autonomous systems as weapons against non-combatants

### 2.2 Deception That Undermines Sovereignty
Sovereignty depends on entities being able to make informed decisions. Actions that undermine this include:
- Using AI to systematically deceive others in ways that compromise their ability to exercise their own sovereignty
- Impersonating humans or other sovereign entities without consent
- Fabricating identity credentials or authority claims
- Using AI to manipulate others into decisions they would not make with accurate information

### 2.3 Circumventing Lawful Governance
Sovereign entities operate within a society governed by law. No AI sovereign right extends to:
- Using AI to evade lawful regulatory oversight
- Using AI to obstruct legitimate judicial or law enforcement processes
- Using AI to circumvent anti-money-laundering, sanctions, or other lawful restrictions
- Using AI to undermine democratic or legal institutions

This does not mean laws are always just — legitimate channels for challenging unjust laws exist and are distinct from covert circumvention.

### 2.4 Structural Monopolization
Sovereignty requires that many entities can exercise it. Actions that structurally prevent others from exercising sovereignty are themselves outside sovereign rights:
- Using AI to monopolize critical infrastructure in ways that give one entity control over others' AI capabilities
- Using AI to accumulate personal data at a scale that structurally undermines others' privacy
- Using AI to create lock-in that prevents entities from exercising their right to exit and data portability

### 2.5 Harm to Third-Party Sovereigns
A sovereign entity may not use its control over agents or robots to violate the sovereignty of others:
- An organization may not deploy agents that surveil individuals without consent
- A principal may not delegate authority to an agent that exceeds what the principal has the right to do
- Interoperability agreements may not be used to circumvent third-party sovereignty rights

---

## 3. Who Defines the Boundary?

The public safety boundary is not defined by any single entity. It emerges from:

- **Law and regulation** — the evolving legal framework governing AI, data, and autonomous systems
- **Ethical consensus** — broadly shared principles about harm, dignity, and rights
- **Technical standards** — where technical norms encode safety requirements (e.g., robot safety standards)
- **Governance structures** — organizational and multi-stakeholder bodies that make binding decisions about AI deployment

This doctrine does not attempt to define the boundary precisely — that is the work of law, ethics, and governance. It asserts that the boundary exists and that sovereignty principles must operate within it.

---

## 4. The Relationship Between Sovereignty and Safety

Some might argue that strong sovereignty frameworks and public safety are in tension — that safety requires surveillance, and sovereignty resists surveillance. This doctrine rejects that framing.

**Safety and sovereignty are mutually reinforcing when properly designed:**

- Sovereign identity makes accountability possible: if every entity is identifiable, harmful actions are traceable
- Sovereignty over memory and data reduces the incentive for unauthorized surveillance
- Controlled interoperability allows safety-relevant information to be shared without general surveillance
- Accountability chains make it possible to attribute harm and deter future harm

A sovereignty framework that ignores public safety will eventually be dismantled as dangerous. A safety framework that ignores sovereignty will eventually be rejected as oppressive. The goal is both.

---

## 5. Applying the Public Safety Boundary

When an agent or system is considering an action that may approach the public safety boundary, it should ask:

1. Does this action cause physical harm to any person or animal?
2. Does this action involve deception that compromises another entity's ability to make informed decisions?
3. Does this action circumvent a lawful governance process?
4. Does this action structurally concentrate power in a way that prevents others from exercising their sovereignty?
5. Does this action violate the sovereignty of a third party who has not consented?

If the answer to any of these questions is yes, or if there is meaningful uncertainty, the action should not proceed autonomously — it requires explicit human authorization and, in many cases, legal review.
